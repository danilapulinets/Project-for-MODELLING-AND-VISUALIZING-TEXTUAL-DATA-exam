About the Article
"How Historians Use Visualization: A Corpus-Backed Taxonomy and Analysis for Cross-Disciplinary Practice" (Chen, Zhang, Zheng, Ma, Yuan, EuroVis 2026) is a mixed-methods study examining how historians actually use visualization in their research. The authors analysed 4,142 articles from 6 history and digital humanities journals, extracted 14,021 images, and identified 4,831 visualization instances. Through a collaboratively developed taxonomy and interviews with 11 historians, the study identifies five functional roles of visualization, documents barriers to adoption, and surfaces recurring patterns of misuse.

Why This Article
This article caught my attention because it asks something truly simple: what do historians actually put in their papers, and why? The answer turned out to be more complicated than I expected. Historians overwhelmingly prefer maps, line charts, and bar charts - safe, legible, straightforward forms. Not because they lack imagination, but because every visualization they publish is also an evidentiary claim, and that comes with responsibility.
What was most interesting to me was the misuse section. The examples are not huge failures; they are small, easy mistakes: using an area chart for categorical data, reducing gender to a binary, and encoding time through colour. These are the kinds of choices that look fine at first glance but silently distort the argument. And historians, trained to be suspicious of exactly this kind of silent simplification, are right to be cautious.
The study also revealed a gap I found genuinely surprising: almost every historian interviewed uses visualisation privately - family trees, tables, sketch maps to organise their thinking. But almost none of it gets published. What reaches the reader is only the clean, communicative end product, not the messy, exploratory process behind it. That gap between private practice and public output feels like something worth thinking about.

Critical Reflection
The article's most important lesson for modelling is that historians resist visualization not out of technophobia but out of methodological vigilance. Their resistance is a form of epistemological discipline - the same rigour they apply to any other source. This should make the DH community more humble: before asking why historians aren't using more visualization, we should ask what visualization would need to look like to meet historians' evidentiary standards.
